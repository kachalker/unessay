Findings

The textual analysis performed on the correspondences contained in the Calendar of State Papers Foreign provides a variety of insights into what aspects affected the activities of Elizabeth I's Watchers. These insights are best understood by connecting trends found in the data created by textual analysis to historical events that occurred in England from 1570-1588.

1570: Elizabeth I is Excommunicated

Textual analysis of the state papers revealed an odd increase in the number of mentions relating to spies in 1570. Although spies were only mentioned 6 times in 1570, that is still significant considering that from 1571-1576, spies were mentioned no more than 3 times. The 1570 outlier could be explained by Elizabeth I being excommunicated in 1570 because of the fear that her excommunication would motivate English Catholics to try to remove her from the throne.

1571: The Ridolfi Plot

The Ridolfi Plot does not seem to affect Elizabeth's spy network considering the data garnered from the state papers shows no trends in connection with 1571. However, it is interesting that Ridolfi's name is mentioned in 1577 and 1588, which are the same years that mentions of Catholics spiked. Although this plot appears to have no direct weight on the actions of Elizabeth I's spy network, it is noteworthy that as discussions regarding Catholics rise, mentions of previous Catholic plots also rise, which does suggest that the plots that Elizabeth I's spy network was developed to protect against were often connected with Catholics. Furthermore, the mentions of Ridolfi also directly correlate with mentions of words relating to anxieties which also suggests that Catholic plotters were a common anxiety.

1573: The Throckmorton Plot

Like the Ridolfi Plot, the Throckmorton Plot also appeared to have little direct bearing on Elizabeth I's spy network. Also similar to the Ridolfi Plot, the Throckmorton Plot was mentioned multiple times beginning in 1580, 7 years after the Throckmorton Plot was thwarted. The mentions of Throckmorton also directly correlate with spikes in mentions of anxieties, further suggesting that the Catholic plots that were often the focus of Elizabeth I's spy network were a common anxiety.

1586: The Babington Plot

In the Babington Plot, Thomas Phellipes, the chief cryptographer in Elizabeth's spy network, deciphered the letters exchanged between Mary Queen of Scots and Babington, which was integral in foiling the plot. Textual analysis of the state papers demonstrates Phellipes's importance in uncovering this plot. Before 1585, the year before The Babington Plot, Phellipes's name had only been mentioned. However, in 1585, Phellipes's name was mentioned 12 times, showcasing he was often referenced in what appears to be Elizabeth's Spymaster, Walsingham, making preparations for attempting to catch Mary Queen of Scots committing treason. 1

Also representative of the increased importance of ciphers for Elizabeth I's spy network was the increase of the words cipher and ciphers around the time of The Babington Plot. Although the words cipher and cipher had started to be used more beginning in 1583, the use of the words still peaked in 1587, the year after The Babington Plot was thwarted.

Also interesting is that before 1586, Babington's name had never been mentioned in the correspondences contained in the state papers before. However, after The Babington Plot was stopped, Babington's name was mentioned in 1586 until 1588, the last year examined in this project.

Overall Conclusions

There are a couple of main conclusions that one can make about what the Calendar of State Papers Foreign reveals about Elizabeth I's Watchers. The first is that mentions of Elizabeth's spy network increase with overall anxieties, and the anxieties are directly related to Catholic plotters. Furthermore, the fact that mentions of spies increased in 1570, the year Elizabeth I was excommunicated, also showcases that the actions of Elizabeth's spy network were motivated by Catholic plotters. The connections in this data confirm the common assumption that Elizabeth's spy network was created to stem the anxieties caused by Catholic plotters.

The second noteworthy takeaway from the data is that Elizabeth I's spy network evolved as the world around it evolved. This evolution is most evident in the increase of mentions of ciphers and the chief cryptographer, Thomas Phellipes's, growing importance, as shown by him being mentioned in the state papers. As ciphers became more prevalent, Elizabeth's spy network placed a greater emphasis on them, which led them to eventually being able to foil The Babington Plot.
